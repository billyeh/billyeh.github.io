First you have Facebook Messenger, for the boomer generation and friends from school and church. On Messenger, not only do you have DMs, but you have group messages. Let's say you happen to be in a 30 person group. It might've started out small, maybe five or so people. After while, it started growing. Friends added friends, and life happens.

Now you have a 30 person group. But that's a minor issue, compared to what comes next. Imagine planning an event, but only half of the group is interested. You'll need to make another group, with just those 15 people, to avoid spamming the rest of the people. Before you know it, someone else will be planning another event. This time, 10 out of 30 people are interested. You know the drill. The absurdity of this situation is easily apparent to anyone who tries the math. (30 choose 5) works about to just about 142506 different groups. And that's just the number of possibilities with five-person groups :) (The math for the number of possible groups without restricting to a certain group size is left to the aspiring and interested reader.)

The same thing happens with Whatsapp.

Then you download Discord, since that's what younger folks and gamers use.

You probably have Slack still installed because some official organization wanted to use it back when it was cool.

Before you know it, you have WeChat and Line as well.

But why?

It's hardly worth speculating why we're all buried under our own mountain or hill of messages and comments. Behind this combinatorial explosion of messaging apps, messages, and people is a set of perverse incentives. I'm not attempting to demonize any person or company, but it's a fact that the more engagement an app or website has, the more value it has to business interests.

Those little messages, with their addictive little sounds, animations, and content, have been fine-tuned over the last decade. Thousands of engineers, data scientists, UX researchers, advertisers, and goodness knows how many more people, have been hard at work tuning them. The dopamine hits from each notification are undeniable. Companies have spent billions of dollars optimizing their UX and content to drag consumers into these apps and websites. A company that actively tries to reduce the time a consumer spends on their platform, is a bankrupt company.
